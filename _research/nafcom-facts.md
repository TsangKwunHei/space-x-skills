# NAFCOM Cost Study - Fact Bank

Source document: "Falcon 9 Launch Vehicle NAFCOM Cost Estimates," August 2011, NASA Associate Deputy Administrator for Policy (file: 586023main_8-3-11_NAFCOM.pdf, 9 pages).

## Topic: Three Cost Estimates ($3.977B / $1.695B / $443M)

- [page 2] "The activity estimated Falcon 9 would cost $3.977B based on NASA environment/culture."
- [page 2] "NAFCOM predicted $1.695B when all technical inputs were adjusted to a more commercial development approach."
- [page 2] The objective was to estimate Falcon 9 development cost using two approaches: (1) traditional NASA environment/culture, and (2) a more commercial development culture approach.
- [page 2] The original analysis was performed late 2010-early 2011 using the NASA-Air Force Cost Model (NAFCOM); results were provided to ESMD in November 2010.
- [page 2] Original results were used for Appendix B of the "Commercial Market Assessment for Crew and Cargo Systems" and for the Deputy Administrator's presentation at the National Space Symposium.
- [page 2] After a trip to the SpaceX facility, NASA inputted revised data into NAFCOM and "a different output was the result."
- [page 5] Falcon 9 NAFCOM Cost Estimate Comparison (All Costs in FY2010 $M): Initial Estimate Cost Plus Fee Total = $1,695 (SpaceX approach) and $3,977 (NASA approach); Updated Estimate Firm Fixed Price Total = $443 and Updated Cost Plus Fee Total = $1,383.
- [page 5] The updated $443M Firm Fixed Price total "Excludes Program Support costs (Firm Fixed Price only)," "Excludes Contingency costs (Firm Fixed Price only)," and "Excludes Contractor fee (Firm Fixed Price only)."
- [page 8] Initial NASA-approach $3,977M total decomposes to: Stage One (incl. engines) $1,741M; Stage Two (incl. engine) $651M; Fee (12.5%) $298M; Program Support (10%) $263M; Contingency (30% Vehicle, 10% Engine) $741M; Vehicle Level Integration (8%) $282M.
- [page 8] Initial SpaceX-approach $1,659M total (note: paper headline rounds to $1,695M) decomposes to: Stage One $701M; Stage Two $343M; Fee $130M; Program Support $111M; Contingency $262M; Vehicle Level Integration $111M. Vehicle weight = 45,600 lbs.
- [page 9] Updated Firm Fixed Price $443.4M total decomposes to: Stage One $298.0M ($188.7M DDT&E + $109.3M for 2 Test Flt Units); Stage Two $112.6M ($89.0M + $23.6M); Fee $0; Program Support $0; Contingency $0; Vehicle Level Integration $32.8M ($22.2M + $10.6M).
- [page 9] Updated Cost Plus Fee $1,382.7M total decomposes to: Stage One $588.9M; Stage Two $244.4M; Fee $104.2M; Program Support $93.7M; Contingency $284.9M; Vehicle Level Integration $66.7M.
- [page 4] Both the initial and updated cost estimates "are not NASA budget estimates" - they "Do not include all costs that would be included in a NASA project budget" and "Are schedule and time phasing independent."

## Topic: Six Driver Categories (priority-ordered)

The model input assumptions that drive the difference between Firm Fixed Price and Cost Plus Fee estimates are listed in priority order (page 6); "Order represents relative weighting":

- [page 6] 1 - Acquisition Strategy: Firm Fixed Price = "No Oversight"; Cost Plus Fee = "Oversight."
- [page 6] 2 - Requirements Stability: FFP = "Stable"; CPF = "Unstable."
- [page 6] 3 - Team Efficiency [spelled "Effeciency" in source]: FFP = "Efficient"; CPF = "Less Efficient."
- [page 6] 4 - Management Structure: FFP = "Lean"; CPF = "Less Lean."
- [page 6] 5 - Early Phase studies / Sys. Engineering: FFP = "Disciplined"; CPF = "Less Disciplined."
- [page 6] 6 - Funding Commitment: FFP = "Fixed"; CPF = "Annual."

## Topic: $3-to-$5 Subcontractor Multiplier

- [page 3] "Organizational complexity – SpaceX estimates that every dollar sent out of the company actually costs between $3 and $5 based on subcontractor overhead and profit." (Verbatim quote from the SpaceX-attributed "primary factors" list.)
- [page 3] This is listed as factor #2 of three SpaceX-cited cost-efficiency drivers.

## Topic: Acquisition Strategy (oversight vs no-oversight)

- [page 6] First and highest-weighted modeling input. FFP modeled with "No Oversight"; CPF modeled with "Oversight."
- [page 9] Cost-plus-fee acquisition reflects fee + program support + contingency line items; firm-fixed-price acquisition "reflects a space act agreement approach" with those three line items zeroed out.
- [page 5] Going from Cost Plus Fee to Firm Fixed Price (with all other corrections from the SpaceX visit) drops the total from $1,383M to $443M - a roughly $940M delta attributed largely to acquisition strategy and the items it excludes.

## Topic: Requirements Stability (stable vs unstable)

- [page 6] Second-highest-weighted driver. FFP assumes "Stable" requirements; CPF assumes "Unstable" requirements.
- [page 6] Implication: requirements churn is modeled as a primary cost inflator in the traditional NASA/CPF environment.

## Topic: Team Efficiency / Lean Management

- [page 6] Team Efficiency is the #3 driver: FFP = "Efficient," CPF = "Less Efficient."
- [page 6] Management Structure is the #4 driver: FFP = "Lean," CPF = "Less Lean."
- [page 3] Operational meaning per SpaceX: "reducing the total workforce; number of management layers and infrastructure can substantially reduce DDT&E costs when compared to traditional NASA environment/culture."
- [page 3] "Workforce - Total vehicle DDT&E costs are primarily a product of the total workforce needed to accomplish the effort (SpaceX workforce numbers substantiate their development cost claims)." Listed as cost-efficiency factor #1.
- [page 3] "Infrastructure - Total infrastructure required for the DDT&E effort and infrastructure utilization percentage." Listed as cost-efficiency factor #3.

## Topic: Funding Commitment (annual vs fixed-price)

- [page 6] Sixth driver: FFP = "Fixed" funding commitment; CPF = "Annual" funding commitment.
- [page 9] FFP modeling zeros out Fee (12.5%), Program Support (10%), and Contingency (30% Vehicle, 10% Engine) line items, while CPF carries all three.
- [page 4] Updated estimates "provided both Cost Plus Fee and Firm Fixed Price approaches and included two flight unites [sic]"; the original estimates "Provided only a Cost Plus Fee approach" and "Included a single first flight unit."
- [page 9] Cost-plus-fee approach "include[s] fee, program support, and contingency where firm fixed price acquisition reflects a space act agreement approach."

## Topic: Falcon 1 Heritage / Off-the-shelf Hardware

- [page 5] Assumptions inserted after the SpaceX visit: "More heritage from Falcon 1 and use of off-the-shelf hardware than the initial information."
- [page 5] "Nine redundant controllers and wiring harnesses were not identified in the initial information."
- [page 5] "Structure weights were included in the electronics."
- [page 5] "Separation subsystem mass was included in structure."
- [page 5] "Interstage (composite material) was included in structures (aluminum-lithium material)."
- [page 9] Updated cost figures are "Based on technical corrections and the additional insight in to the mass summary information as well as hardware heritage gained from a recent trip to the SpaceX facility."

## Topic: Two Flight Units vs One

- [page 4] The original estimates "Included a single first flight unit." The updated estimates "included two flight unites [sic]" (i.e., two test flight units).
- [page 5] "Falcon 9 test program initially included only one test flight unit" - listed among the assumptions revised after the SpaceX visit.
- [page 9] Updated FFP: 2 Test Flight Units add $109.3M (Stage One) + $23.6M (Stage Two) + $10.6M (Vehicle Level Integration) = ~$143.6M to the $443.4M total.
- [page 9] Updated CPF: 2 Test Flight Units add $218.3M (Stage One) + $59.6M (Stage Two) plus prorated fee/support/contingency, totaling $457.9M of the $1,382.7M total.
- [page 8] In the initial estimate, a single flight unit added $393M (NASA approach) or $131M (SpaceX approach) on top of DDT&E.

## Topic: SpaceX Practices Observed at Hawthorne

- [page 2] "Working with different inputs from the preliminary data provided by SpaceX after a trip to the SpaceX facility, NASA recently inputted revised data into the NAFCOM model and a different output was the result."
- [page 4] "NASA personnel subsequently visited the Space X facilities and spoke with SpaceX personnel about the inputs and results of the initial modeling effort."
- [page 3] "NASA did not perform a detailed analysis to explain the significant differences between the cost estimates and SpaceX actual costs. However, SpaceX attributed their cost efficiencies to a few primary factors": (1) Workforce, (2) Organizational complexity ($3-$5 multiplier on outsourced dollars), (3) Infrastructure.
- [page 3] Conclusion drawn from those factors: "reducing the total workforce; number of management layers and infrastructure can substantially reduce DDT&E costs when compared to traditional NASA environment/culture."
- [page 9] The visit produced "additional insight in to the mass summary information as well as hardware heritage" - revealing redundant controllers/wiring harnesses, electronics-embedded structure weight, separation subsystem mass, and a composite (vs aluminum-lithium) interstage that the initial NASA model had not captured.
